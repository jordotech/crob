BIOGRAPHY

Malcolm Clark Robertson was born in Navasota, Texas in 1955. He lived and worked as an artist, textile and furniture designer, and educator in New York City during the 1980s, until his death from AIDS related complications in 1991. Robertson’s work was shown in solo exhibitions at Galerie Ton Peek in Amsterdam and International With Monument Gallery in New York and was included in group exhibitions at White Columns Gallery, The Queens Museum, and Art et Industrie Gallery, all in New York City. He created a collection of silkscreened textiles, Photosilks, for Inger McCabe Elliot, photojournalist and founder of China Seas Fabrics. Robertson taught textile design at the New School for Social Research (1983-1986) and at Parsons School of Design (1983-1990). His work is held in the permanent collection of the Cooper Hewitt Smithsonian Design Museum in New York City.

Robertson’s visual art is often large in scale, and typically employs a process of layered silkscreened images on dyed linen, which are then worked into with pigment, embroidery and other mixed media. While Robertson’s imagery was drawn from diverse sources, a First World War-era book on facial reconstructive surgery serves as a foundational text for his work. He was interested in the ways in which physical suffering is understood visually and culturally, and in creating a contemplative space where representations of death, disfigurement, sex and beauty might coexist in all of their fraught contingencies.
